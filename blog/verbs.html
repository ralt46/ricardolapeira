Verbs

When learning a language, we are usually taught grammatical structures like nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and so on. All of these are unique in their own way. For me, verbs stand out for a particular reason: they are enablers of possibilities.

Other grammatical elements also play important roles, but verbs are the ones that truly enable things to occur. The subject cannot really do something unless a verb establishes that possibility. The kid cannot throw the ball unless throwing is an option. The dog cannot bark unless the concept of barking is defined by the language.

Yes, this is obvious. So why does it matter? In our daily lives, we tend to forget that our language defines the boundaries of how we see the world. If we lack a certain verb within our repertoire, the action it enables will probably lie outside our possibilities. When our repertoire consists of only a few verbs, our actions will be very limited.

When we lack the concept associated with a verb, it is very unlikely that we can perform it. Similarly, when the concept is embedded into our language, the action becomes more likely to occur.

There usually comes a point in our lives when we stop learning new things about language or thinking about how we use our language. We are passive users of the words that are given to us. This does not have to be the case.

Being proactive about our language can help us realize that there is an entire world of verbs that exists out there within our reach, some of which already exist, some of which still need to be invented.
