ContentOps

While terms like DevOps and DesignOps have become well-known, ContentOps is still less well-known.

DevOps, in the broad sense of the word, refers to many things related to building software. For developers, it has a specific connotation about a particular set of activities. Design, as its name suggests, encompasses design-related activities. These have seen an important surge in the past decades as companies formalize their design processes. It is sometimes said that when companies reach a stage of maturity, it makes sense to talk about DesignOps. Although not as popular as DevOps, many companies have, in one way or another, a form of DesignOps, even if they don’t explicitly use that name.

Meanwhile, ContentOps remains more of a niche concept. It might be because the idea of content itself is hard to pin down. Also, it is probably the case, like with DesignOps, that most companies don’t really get to a point where referring to content processes makes sense. That is a shame.

I believe that many companies would benefit from integrating ContentOps into their way of thinking about their processes. In practice, content operations cover all the different activities associated with creating content. This includes early stages, like ideation, to more mature ones like testing and refining. It covers writing texts, not as something you do to fill up a website or deliver a pointless newsletter, but rather something that strengthens your brand and helps achieve specific outcomes. To be fair, some of these activities only make sense for companies with bigger budgets and more generous timelines.

In reality, ContentOps is probably not the thing that will make a company go bankrupt. However, it can definitely be the reason why peak potential is not reached. The best companies I’ve worked with take ContentOps very seriously, not because they have money to spend, but because they understand that having a clear and structured way of thinking about content operations delivers real, tangible value, both in terms of the bottom line and in how the organization works.

The best marketing pipelines, digital product development teams, and knowledge-focused organizations all take ContentOps very seriously.
